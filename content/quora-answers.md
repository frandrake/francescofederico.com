## 1. What is agentic AI and how is it different from generative AI?

**Question to search:** "What is agentic AI and how is it different from generative AI?"

**Answer:**

This is a distinction that matters enormously for business leaders, and one I find is frequently misunderstood.

Generative AI creates. It produces text, images, code, and video based on prompts. Think of ChatGPT drafting an email or Midjourney generating a campaign visual. It's powerful, but it's fundamentally reactive — it waits for you to ask, produces an output, and stops.

Agentic AI acts. It perceives its environment, reasons about what needs to happen, takes action, and learns from the outcome. The difference isn't incremental — it's architectural. An agentic system doesn't just answer your question; it identifies that the question needs asking in the first place, executes a multi-step plan to address it, and adapts when circumstances change.

Consider a practical example. Starbucks' Deep Brew platform doesn't simply generate marketing copy when prompted. It continuously analyses customer behaviour, inventory levels, weather patterns, and local preferences to autonomously orchestrate personalised offers across millions of customers — adjusting in real time without waiting for a marketer to intervene. That's the shift from generative to agentic.

In my work, I use what I call the PACE model to evaluate agentic capabilities: Perception (can it sense its environment?), Action (can it execute decisions?), Cognition (can it reason about complex problems?), and Evolution (can it learn and improve over time?). Most tools marketed as "AI agents" today score well on one or two dimensions but fall short on the others.

The practical implication for organisations is significant. Generative AI typically augments individual tasks — a copywriter works faster, an analyst summarises data more quickly. Agentic AI transforms workflows and, ultimately, operating models. It demands new governance frameworks, new team structures, and new ways of measuring performance.

We're still in the early stages of the agentic era, but the trajectory is clear. Leaders who understand this distinction now will be far better positioned than those who treat every AI capability as interchangeable.

## 2. What skills do CMOs need in the age of AI?

**Question to search:** "What skills do CMOs need in the age of AI?"

**Answer:**

Having spent considerable time studying this question — both through my own experience and through research for my book — I'd argue the required skillset has shifted more dramatically in the past two years than in the preceding decade.

The traditional CMO competencies haven't disappeared. Brand stewardship, commercial acumen, and storytelling still matter. But they're no longer sufficient. Here's what I believe distinguishes effective marketing leaders in the agentic era:

**Architectural thinking.** CMOs must now design hybrid organisations where humans and AI systems collaborate. This means understanding not just what AI can do, but how to structure teams, workflows, and decision rights around human-machine partnerships. Walmart's approach to demand sensing, where AI handles pattern recognition at scale while merchants retain strategic pricing decisions, exemplifies this well.

**Data fluency — not data science.** You don't need to build models, but you absolutely need to understand how data flows through your organisation, where the gaps are, and how AI systems use (and misuse) it. The CMOs I see struggling most are those who still delegate all data conversations to their analytics teams.

**Governance instinct.** When AI systems can autonomously generate and deploy creative at scale — as Mondelez is now doing with its Publicis/Accenture-built platform — the CMO becomes the chief accountability officer for brand safety, ethical messaging, and regulatory compliance. This isn't a compliance exercise; it's a leadership competency.

**Commercial translation.** The ability to build a credible business case for AI investment, articulate ROI in language the CFO respects, and sequence initiatives from pilot to scale. Too many marketing leaders still pitch AI in terms of innovation rather than value creation.

**Change leadership.** Perhaps most critically, the CMO must lead cultural transformation. AI adoption fails far more often due to organisational resistance than technological limitations.

I've developed a competency framework covering twelve capabilities across four domains that I use to assess CMO readiness. You can find it in [*The Agentic CMO*](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B0FDXCZSS3), along with practical approaches to developing each one.

## 3. How should companies structure their marketing teams to work with AI?

**Question to search:** "How should companies structure their marketing teams to work with AI?"

**Answer:**

This is one of the most consequential questions in marketing leadership right now, and there's no single answer — but there are clear patterns emerging from organisations that are getting it right.

Through my research, I've identified three architectural models that work:

**The Hub-and-Spoke Model.** A centralised AI centre of excellence develops capabilities, sets standards, and provides tools, while individual marketing teams (brand, performance, content) apply them in context. This works well for large, diversified organisations where consistency matters but local application varies. It's the model most enterprise marketing organisations adopt first.

**The Embedded Model.** AI specialists sit directly within functional marketing teams rather than in a central unit. This accelerates adoption and ensures AI solutions are tightly integrated with real workflows. The risk is fragmentation — without coordination, you end up with redundant tools and inconsistent governance.

**The Hybrid Collaborative Model.** This combines elements of both: a lightweight central team handles governance, vendor relationships, and shared infrastructure, while embedded practitioners drive day-to-day integration. This is where the most sophisticated organisations — those genuinely operating at scale — tend to converge.

Regardless of model, several principles hold:

First, new roles are essential. Creative technologists, automation producers, and AI operations managers aren't luxuries — they're the connective tissue between your people and your platforms. Mondelez's AI-driven creative engine, for instance, required entirely new roles such as brand safety reviewers and creative operations leads to function effectively.

Second, redesign workflows before inserting AI. Simply bolting AI onto legacy processes amplifies inefficiencies. Ask: "If we were designing this function from scratch today, knowing what AI can do, how would we structure it?"

Third, rethink procurement. Traditional agency models built around billable hours and campaign cadences are increasingly misaligned with AI-enabled workflows. Reward outcomes, not inputs.

The companies pulling ahead — Starbucks, Walmart, Coca-Cola — share a common trait: they treat organisational design as a strategic capability, not an afterthought.

## 5. How do you build a business case for AI in marketing?

**Question to search:** "How do you build a business case for AI in marketing?"

**Answer:**

This is where most marketing leaders get stuck — and where most AI initiatives die. Having helped build these cases and observed what works across organisations, I'd offer this framework.

**Stop leading with cost savings.** Yes, efficiency gains are real — Mondelez is achieving 30–50% reductions in creative production costs through its AI platform. But if your entire business case rests on doing the same things cheaper, you'll underinvest and underwhelm. The CFO will approve a modest pilot, it'll deliver modest results, and AI will be filed under "nice to have."

**Build a value stack with three layers:**

*Layer 1 — Efficiency.* This is your credibility layer. Identify high-volume, repetitive marketing tasks where AI delivers measurable time and cost savings. Content versioning, asset production, reporting automation. Quantify these honestly. This funds the programme and builds organisational confidence.

*Layer 2 — Enhancement.* AI doesn't just do existing things faster; it does them better. Personalisation at scale, real-time creative optimisation, predictive audience segmentation. Starbucks' Deep Brew platform demonstrates this: it doesn't merely automate offer delivery — it continuously optimises across millions of customer contexts simultaneously, improving both customer experience and commercial performance.

*Layer 3 — Strategic value.* This is where the real returns live. What new capabilities does AI unlock that were previously impossible? New market entry, new product development informed by real-time demand signals, new business models. Walmart's demand sensing capabilities allow it to respond to market shifts faster than competitors — that's competitive advantage, not cost reduction.

**Sequence ruthlessly.** Start with use cases that are high-frequency, commercially impactful, and technically feasible. Build a portfolio that delivers quick wins (Layer 1) while investing in longer-term strategic capabilities (Layers 2 and 3).

**Speak the CFO's language.** Frame investments in terms of cost-per-outcome evolution: cost-per-engagement, cost-per-conversion, cost-per-incremental-sale. This reframes marketing AI as a revenue engine, not a cost centre.

I detail the full investment architecture, including templates and financial modelling approaches, in [*The Agentic CMO*](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B0FDXCZSS3).
