# The AI-First Company: What It Really Means and Why Most Organisations Are Getting It Wrong

**Why Duolingo, Shopify, and Box are rewriting the rules — and five imperatives for leaders who want to keep up**

---

*This article was originally published in [Chronicles of Change](https://chroniclesofchange.substack.com/), my weekly newsletter on enterprise AI strategy.*

---

Duolingo, Shopify and now Box are just a few prominent names of companies recently declaring the pivot to "AI-First Company". But what does it truly mean to be AI-first? And more critically, how can organisations navigate this transformation without losing their soul in the process?

## It's Not About Efficiency. It's About Capability.

Successful AI adoption isn't just about automating existing workflows — it's increasingly becoming about unlocking capabilities that were previously unimaginable.

If a particular skill suddenly became 100 times cheaper and more abundant, what would you do differently?

AI is making this reality across countless domains — from software development to market analysis, from customer service to strategic planning. Hyper-personalised content marketing is already the primary beneficiary of this shift.

The temptation is to view AI through the lens of efficiency: doing the same things faster and cheaper. But this misses the transformative potential entirely.

> AI-first organisations are asking fundamentally different questions: What couldn't we do before? What problems were too complex to solve? What opportunities were invisible to us?

## The Counterintuitive Truth: AI Makes Organisations More Human

Perhaps the most counterintuitive insight from Box's research — and one that aligns with my own observations — is that becoming AI-first actually makes organisations more human, not less.

The most sophisticated AI implementations aren't replacing human judgement; they're amplifying it. When mundane tasks are automated, humans are liberated to focus on what they do best: creative problem-solving, relationship building, and strategic thinking.

The challenge lies in designing this partnership intentionally. The sweet spot lies in augmented intelligence — where AI handles data processing and pattern recognition while humans provide context, ethics, and strategic direction.

## You Can't Run Modern Software on Legacy Hardware

Here's where many organisations stumble: they treat AI as a bolt-on technology rather than a foundational capability.

Box's framework correctly identifies that AI-first companies must redesign their entire operating system — from data governance to decision-making processes.

Think of it this way: you wouldn't run modern software on 1990s hardware. Similarly, you can't achieve AI transformation with legacy organisational structures.

This means rethinking everything from how teams are organised to how success is measured.

Data, in particular, transforms from a byproduct of operations to a strategic asset. Unstructured data — those contracts, emails, presentations, and documents that comprise 80% of enterprise information — suddenly becomes a goldmine of insights when AI can process it effectively.

## Three Pitfalls That Derail AI-First Transformations

The path to becoming AI-first is littered with cautionary tales. Three pitfalls prove particularly treacherous:

### The Automation Trap

Simply digitising broken processes amplifies their inefficiencies. Before implementing AI, ask: "If we were designing this function from scratch today, knowing AI's capabilities, how would we structure it?"

### The Cost-Cutting Myopia

Organisations that view AI primarily as a cost-reduction tool miss its true potential. Yes, efficiency gains are real, but the transformative value lies in new capabilities — entering markets you couldn't before, solving problems that were previously intractable, delivering experiences that were once impossible.

### The Governance Gap

In the rush to implement AI, many organisations neglect the ethical and security frameworks necessary for sustainable deployment. Remember: AI agents can't keep secrets. Your governance model must reflect this reality from day one.

## The Maturity Curve

Box's maturity model resonates with patterns I've observed across industries. Most organisations are still at the first level — using AI for basic information retrieval and insights. But the trajectory is clear: we're moving toward a future where AI systems will collaborate autonomously while humans provide strategic oversight.

## Five Imperatives for Leaders

Based on both Box's research and my own experience, five imperatives stand out:

**1. Appoint an AI Transformation Leader.** This isn't a part-time role or a committee responsibility. You need someone with the authority and vision to drive change across the entire organisation.

**2. Invest in AI-Native Infrastructure.** Ensure your technology stack is truly interoperable and can support multiple AI models. The pace of innovation means you can't afford to be locked into a single vendor or approach.

**3. Develop Your Talent Strategy Now.** The war for AI-literate talent is intensifying. But equally important is upskilling your existing workforce. Create a culture where experimentation is encouraged and AI fluency is developed organically.

**4. Start with High-Impact Use Cases.** Look for areas where work is both frequent and requires critical thinking. These "transformational use cases" offer the greatest return on investment and build momentum for broader adoption.

**5. Embed Ethics from the Start.** Don't treat governance as an afterthought. Build transparent, accountable AI systems that enhance trust rather than eroding it.

## The Window Is Closing

Here's the uncomfortable truth: the window for becoming AI-first is closing rapidly. Box's research shows that 60% of companies expect to achieve AI transformation within two years. Those that lag behind won't just be less efficient — they'll be fundamentally unable to compete.

> Imagine trying to compete in today's market without internet connectivity or mobile capabilities. That's the disadvantage non-AI-first companies will face by 2027.

The question isn't whether to transform, but how quickly you can evolve while maintaining your organisational values and human-centricity.

Success comes not from the technology itself, but from leaders who view AI as a catalyst for reimagining what's possible. The most exciting aspect of becoming AI-first isn't the efficiency gains or cost savings — it's the opportunity to solve problems we couldn't even articulate before.

Are you prepared to fundamentally reimagine your organisation's capabilities, or will you simply automate yesterday's processes? The choice you make today will determine your relevance tomorrow.
